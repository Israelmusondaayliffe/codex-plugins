# Drafting From Evidence

## Purpose

Build the deliverable from verified claims rather than writing first and searching for citations afterward.

## Preconditions

Confirm that the work has:

- A clear brief or request.
- A source ledger or equivalent source record.
- A claim ledger or explicit claim assessment.
- A known audience and decision.

If important claims remain unresolved, narrow the draft or return to research.

## Workflow

### 1. Choose The Decision Structure

Organize the document around what the reader needs to understand or decide. Do not mirror the order in which sources were found.

A practical default is:

1. Decision or answer.
2. Most important evidence.
3. Reasoning and alternatives.
4. Risks, conflicts, and limits.
5. Recommended action.

Follow the user's required template when one exists.

### 2. Draft From Supported Claims

Use supported claims as factual statements with nearby citations. Keep wording no broader than the evidence.

### 3. Label Analysis

Signal when the text interprets evidence:

- "This suggests..."
- "The likely explanation is..."
- "I infer..."
- "Taken together, the evidence points to..."

Do not hide inference behind a citation.

### 4. State Recommendations As Judgments

Name the recommendation, the evidence behind it, and the assumptions that make it reasonable. Explain what would change the decision.

### 5. Preserve Conflicts And Limits

Include material disagreement, missing data, stale evidence, inaccessible sources, or scope limits where they affect the answer.

Do not bury important uncertainty in a generic disclaimer at the end.

### 6. Cite Precisely

- Put each citation near the claim it supports.
- Prefer direct links to primary or authoritative sources.
- Use multiple citations only when each adds independent support.
- Do not attach one citation to a paragraph containing several unrelated claims.
- Check that quotations preserve the source's meaning.

### 7. Write For The Audience

Use the reader's language and required level of detail. Lead with the outcome. Keep methods and source notes available without making the main document read like a research diary.

## Quality Check

Read the draft once with each lens:

1. Brief: does it answer the requested question?
2. Evidence: does every factual claim trace to support?
3. Reasoning: are assumptions and alternatives visible?
4. Reader: can the intended user act on it?
5. Language: is the prose plain, specific, and free of inflated claims?
